📂Opening the File

Some numbers lie.
This one did.

Revenue was up 18%. Champagne was almost opened.

But somewhere in the finance team, someone noticed something that didn't add up. Sales were growing. Customers were buying. The dashboard looked healthy.

Yet profits were quietly... shrinking.

If CartPulse Nigeria was selling more than ever — why was less money hitting the bottom line?

I opened the data to find out.

· · ·
🔍The Investigation

20,000 rows. Three cities.
One uncomfortable truth.

But before I could find answers, I had to earn the right to trust the data.

The raw file was a disaster. Cities spelled five different ways. Dates in four different formats. Products assigned to wrong categories. 200 duplicate orders hiding in plain sight. Negative profits that made no mathematical sense.

Eleven data quality issues in total.

I documented every single one. Cleaned every single one. Wrote a formal Data Quality Report. And only then — did I let the data speak.

What it said next surprised everyone.

⚡Finding One

Electronics was carrying the entire business on its back.

₦1.57 billion in revenue. The highest of any category. The boardroom loved it.

But when I looked at the profit margin — Electronics was keeping only ₦38 out of every ₦100 it made. The rest? Gone to costs.

Meanwhile Fashion was sitting quietly in second place. Less revenue, yes. But keeping ₦52 out of every ₦100.

38.1%
Electronics margin — highest revenue, lowest efficiency
52.3%
Fashion margin — nearly matching Electronics profit on far less revenue

Fashion was almost as profitable as Electronics — on hundreds of millions less in revenue. Nobody had noticed.

The business wasn't broken. It was betting everything on its most expensive category while its most efficient one was being ignored.

CartPulse was spending its marketing budget promoting Electronics while their most efficient product was collecting dust on a digital shelf.

🗺️Finding Three

Port Harcourt leads.
But not for the reason you think.

Port Harcourt had the highest revenue and profit of all three cities. The natural assumption was that customers there spent more.

I checked the average order value. Then checked it again.

All three cities had virtually identical values. Port Harcourt: ₦141,096. Abuja: ₦142,936. Lagos: ₦139,875.

Port Harcourt didn't win because its customers were better. It won because it had more of them.

Lagos and Abuja weren't underperforming markets. They were underleveraged ones.

⚖️The Verdict

Revenue was never the problem.

CartPulse Nigeria is not a struggling business. It is a business with a hidden structural problem.

Electronics is generating revenue but bleeding margin. Fashion is the efficiency story nobody was telling. And while the top line grew, the customer base was quietly eroding underneath.

What was earned versus what was kept — that was the real story all along.

· · ·
💡The Recommendations

Five things I told
the executive team.

1
Stop treating Electronics as the hero
It is the workhorse, not the profit engine. Protect it, yes. But stop betting everything on the category with the thinnest margin.
2
Fashion deserves a seat at the strategy table
The Zara Midi Dress at 61.1% margin is more efficient than your entire Electronics catalogue. Scale it. Market it. Let it work.
3
Invest in Lagos and Abuja
Since margins are identical across all cities, increasing order volume in Lagos and Abuja would directly grow profits without additional complexity.
4
Protect your Champions
The 547 Champions and Loyal customers are the backbone of CartPulse's revenue. A loyalty programme costs a fraction of what losing them would.
5
Launch an At Risk win-back campaign — now
247 customers are At Risk. They haven't churned yet. A personalised campaign today costs far less than acquiring replacements tomorrow.
